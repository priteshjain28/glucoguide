Health library

Symptoms, causes, what to do, and trusted resources — for you and your family. General education, not personal medical advice.

Get emergency help immediately for: confusion or unconsciousness, seizures, vomiting that won't stop, trouble breathing, chest pain, fruity-smelling breath, severe dehydration, or very high sugar that is not coming down. Family: if the person can't swallow safely, do not force food or drink — call emergency services.

Low blood sugar (hypoglycemia)

Symptoms to recognize
  • Shakiness, sweating, fast heartbeat
  • Hunger, nausea
  • Dizziness, weakness, blurred vision
  • Irritability, anxiety, confusion
  • Severe: slurred speech, seizures, unconsciousness

Generally suspected when glucose drops below about 70 mg/dL (3.9 mmol/L). Some people stop feeling early symptoms ("hypo unawareness") — tell your doctor if that happens.

Common causes
  • Too much insulin or sulfonylurea-type tablets for the food eaten
  • Skipped or delayed meals
  • More exercise than usual, especially without adjusting food
  • Alcohol, particularly on an empty stomach
What to do (the 15-15 approach many care plans use)
  • Take about 15 g of fast-acting carbs — e.g. glucose tablets, ½ cup fruit juice, or 1 tbsp sugar/honey
  • Wait 15 minutes and recheck glucose
  • Repeat if still low; once recovered, eat a small snack with protein if the next meal is far away
  • Tell your care team about repeated lows — medication may need adjusting

Family members: learn where the fast-acting glucose is kept. If prescribed, learn how to use glucagon. Never give food/drink to someone who is unconscious.

High blood sugar (hyperglycemia)

Symptoms to recognize
  • Increased thirst and frequent urination
  • Fatigue, blurred vision, headache
  • Slow-healing cuts, frequent infections
  • Warning signs of emergency (DKA): nausea/vomiting, stomach pain, fruity breath, deep rapid breathing, confusion
Common causes
  • More carbohydrate than the plan, or sugary drinks
  • Missed medication or insulin doses
  • Illness, infection, or stress (these raise glucose even with normal eating)
  • Less activity than usual; some medications like steroids
What to do
  • Drink water; take medications as prescribed
  • A light walk can help if you feel well — but if glucose is above ~240–250 mg/dL, check ketones if you can, and do not exercise if ketones are present
  • Follow your sick-day plan when ill; contact your care team if highs persist over days, since medication may need adjusting

Living well with diabetes

HbA1c and common targets

HbA1c reflects average glucose over ~3 months. A common target for many adults is below 7%, but your doctor may set a different one based on your age, health, and risk of lows. Common monitoring rhythm: HbA1c every 3–6 months, plus annual eye exam, kidney check (urine/creatinine), foot exam, cholesterol, and blood pressure review.

Sick-day rules
  • Never stop insulin without your doctor's instruction, even if not eating much
  • Check glucose more often (every 2–4 hours); check ketones if high
  • Sip water or sugar-free fluids constantly; if you can't eat, your care team may advise carb-containing fluids
  • Call your care team for repeated vomiting, ketones, or glucose staying very high
Foot care
  • Check feet daily for cuts, blisters, redness, swelling
  • Wash and dry carefully, especially between toes; moisturize tops/soles but not between toes
  • Never walk barefoot; wear well-fitting shoes; trim nails straight across
  • See a doctor promptly for any sore that doesn't heal
Complications and prevention

Long-term high glucose can affect eyes (retinopathy), kidneys (nephropathy), nerves (neuropathy), heart and blood vessels. The good news: keeping glucose, blood pressure, and cholesterol in target, not smoking, and attending screening checks dramatically lowers these risks. Your time-in-range and HbA1c trends in this app are exactly what these screenings build on.

For family members and caregivers
  • Learn the signs of low sugar and where the fast-acting glucose is kept
  • Know the emergency contact and doctor listed in Settings
  • Support, don't police — ask how you can help with meals, walks, and gym consistency
  • Use the Doctor report (Settings) before appointments so visits are data-driven

Your data — backup & restore

Pixel Watch & Google Health

Honest answer: Google's Health Connect (where your Pixel Watch data lives) only opens its data to native Android apps installed from the Play Store — web apps like this one cannot read it, and Google's older web API (Google Fit REST) is shut down for new apps. So automatic watch sync isn't technically possible here yet. What works today: your watch's activity shows in the Fitbit/Google app — log your workout minutes here in seconds via Log → Activity, and your plan's gym item already tracks whether you trained. If this app is ever wrapped as a native Android app, Health Connect sync becomes possible.

Use on your phone

You're running the installable version. In Chrome, tap ⋮ menu → Add to Home screen → Install — GlucoGuide becomes a real app icon, opens full-screen, and works completely offline after the first load. All your data is stored locally on this phone (it never leaves your device). Export a CSV backup now and then, and avoid "Delete browsing data" for this site, since that also deletes locally saved app data.

Before you start

1. Not medical advice. GlucoGuide is a self-tracking and education tool. It does not diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease, and it never replaces your doctor, diabetes educator, or emergency services.

2. Your data stays yours. Everything you enter is stored only on this device. Nothing is uploaded, sold, or shared. You can export or delete all data anytime in Settings.

3. Medication safety. Never change insulin or tablet doses based on this app. Follow your prescription and your clinician's correction plan.

4. Emergencies. For severe low sugar, confusion, vomiting, chest pain, or breathing trouble — seek emergency care immediately, don't consult the app.
